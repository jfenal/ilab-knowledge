**Simone Signoret** (`{{IPA-fr|simɔn siɲɔʁɛ|lang}}`{=mediawiki}; born
**Simone Henriette Charlotte Kaminker**; 25 March 1921 -- 30 September
1985) was a French actress. She received various accolades, including an
[Academy Award](Academy_Awards "wikilink"), three [BAFTA
Awards](British_Academy_Film_Awards "wikilink"), a [César
Award](César_Award "wikilink"), a [Primetime Emmy
Award](Primetime_Emmy_Award "wikilink"), and the [Cannes Film Festival
Award for Best
Actress](Cannes_Film_Festival_Award_for_Best_Actress "wikilink"), in
addition to nominations for two [Golden Globe
Awards](Golden_Globe_Awards "wikilink").

## Early life {#early_life}

Signoret was born Simone Henriette Charlotte Kaminker in
[Wiesbaden](Wiesbaden "wikilink"), Germany, to Georgette (née Signoret)
and André Kaminker. She was the eldest of three children, with two
younger brothers. Her father, a pioneering interpreter who worked in the
[League of Nations](League_of_Nations "wikilink"), was a French-born
army officer from an assimilated and middle-class Polish-Jewish and
Hungarian-Jewish family,[^1][^2] who brought the family to
[Neuilly-sur-Seine](Neuilly-sur-Seine "wikilink") on the outskirts of
Paris. Her mother, Georgette, from whom she acquired her stage name, was
a French Catholic.[^3]

Signoret grew up in Paris in an intellectual atmosphere and studied
English, German and Latin. After completing secondary school during the
[Nazi
occupation](German_military_administration_in_occupied_France_during_World_War_II "wikilink"),
Simone was responsible for supporting her family and forced to take work
as a typist for a French collaborationist newspaper *Les nouveaux
temps*, run by [Jean Luchaire](Jean_Luchaire "wikilink").[^4]

## Career

During the occupation of France, Signoret mixed with an artistic group
of writers and actors who met at the [Café de
Flore](Café_de_Flore "wikilink") in the
[Saint-Germain-des-Prés](Saint-Germain-des-Prés "wikilink") quarter. By
this time, she had developed an interest in acting and was encouraged by
her friends, including her lover [Daniel Gélin](Daniel_Gélin "wikilink")
to follow her ambition. In 1942, she began appearing in bit parts and
was able to earn enough money to support her mother and two brothers as
her father, who was a French patriot, had fled the country in 1940 to
join General [De Gaulle](Charles_de_Gaulle "wikilink") in England. She
took her mother\'s maiden name for the screen to help hide her Jewish
roots.

Signoret\'s sensual features and earthy nature led to type-casting and
she was often seen in roles as a prostitute. She won considerable
attention in *[La Ronde](La_Ronde_(1950_film) "wikilink")* (1950), a
film which was banned briefly in New York City as immoral. She won
further acclaim, including an acting award from the British Film
Academy, for her portrayal of another prostitute in [Jacques
Becker](Jacques_Becker "wikilink")\'s *[Casque
d\'or](Casque_d'or "wikilink")* (1951). She appeared in many French
films during the 1950s, including *[Thérèse
Raquin](Thérèse_Raquin_(1953_film) "wikilink")* (1953), directed by
[Marcel Carné](Marcel_Carné "wikilink"), *[Les
Diaboliques](Les_Diaboliques_(film) "wikilink")* (1954), and *[The
Crucible](The_Crucible_(1957_film) "wikilink")* (*Les Sorcières de
Salem*; 1956), based on [Arthur Miller](Arthur_Miller "wikilink")\'s
*[The Crucible](The_Crucible "wikilink")*. ![Simone Signoret with
[Laurence Harvey](Laurence_Harvey "wikilink") in *[Room at the
Top](Room_at_the_Top_(1959_film) "wikilink")*; the film established her
as an international
actress.](Room_at_the_Top_screenshot.jpg "Simone Signoret with Laurence Harvey in Room at the Top; the film established her as an international actress."){width="200"}

In 1958, Signoret acted in the English independent film *[Room at the
Top](Room_at_the_Top_(1959_film) "wikilink")* (1959), and her
performance won numerous awards, including the [Best Female Performance
Prize](Prix_d'interprétation_féminine "wikilink") at
[Cannes](Cannes_Film_Festival "wikilink") and the [Academy Award for
Best Actress](Academy_Award_for_Best_Actress "wikilink"). She was
offered films in Hollywood, but turned them down for several years,
continuing to work in France and England---for example, with [Laurence
Olivier](Laurence_Olivier "wikilink") in *[Term of
Trial](Term_of_Trial "wikilink")* (1962). She earned another Oscar
nomination for her work on *[Ship of
Fools](Ship_of_Fools_(film) "wikilink")* (1965), appeared in a few other
Hollywood films, and returned to France in 1969.

In 1962, Signoret translated Lillian Hellman\'s play *[The Little
Foxes](The_Little_Foxes "wikilink")* into French for a production in
Paris that ran for six months at the Theatre Sarah-Bernhardt. She played
the Regina role as well. Hellman was displeased with the production,
although the translation was approved by scholars selected by
Hellman.[^5]

Signoret\'s one attempt at Shakespeare, performing [Lady
Macbeth](Macbeth "wikilink") with [Alec
Guinness](Alec_Guinness "wikilink") at the [Royal Court
Theatre](Royal_Court_Theatre "wikilink") in London in 1966 proved to be
ill-advised, with some harsh critics; one referred to her English as
\"impossibly Gallic\".[^6]

Signoret won acclaim for her portrayal of a weary madam in *[Madame
Rosa](Madame_Rosa "wikilink")* (1977) and as an unmarried sister who
unknowingly falls in love with her paralyzed brother via anonymous
correspondence in
*`{{Interlanguage link|Chère inconnue|fr|3=Chère inconnue|lt=I Sent a Letter to my Love}}`{=mediawiki}*
(1980). She continued to appear in many movies before her death in 1985.

## Personal life {#personal_life}

Signoret\'s memoirs *Nostalgia Isn\'t What It Used to Be*, were
published in 1978. She also wrote the novel *Adieu Volodya*, published
in 1985, the year of her death.

Signoret first married filmmaker [Yves
Allégret](Yves_Allégret "wikilink") (1944--1949), with whom she had a
daughter [Catherine Allégret](Catherine_Allégret "wikilink"). Her second
marriage was to the Italian-born French actor [Yves
Montand](Yves_Montand "wikilink") in 1951, a union which lasted until
her death; the couple had no children.

Signoret died of pancreatic cancer in
[Autheuil-Authouillet](Autheuil-Authouillet "wikilink"), France, aged
64. She was buried in [Père Lachaise
Cemetery](Père_Lachaise_Cemetery "wikilink") in Paris, and Yves Montand
later was buried next to her.

Signoret identified as Jewish. She was a supporter of a variety of
Jewish causes, including the [Zionist](Zionism "wikilink") movement and
the [Soviet Jewry movement](Soviet_Jewry_movement "wikilink"). She
maintained relationships with many Israeli leaders and was critical of
antisemitism in the [French Communist
Party](French_Communist_Party "wikilink"). Because she was of
[patrilineal](Who_is_a_Jew? "wikilink") Jewish ancestry and was
therefore not considered Jewish under traditional
[halakha](halakha "wikilink"), there was no religious ceremony at her
funeral.[^7]

## Popular culture {#popular_culture}

-   *Marilyn* (2011) by Sue Glover premiered at the [Citizens\'
    Theatre](Citizens'_Theatre "wikilink"),
    [Glasgow](Glasgow "wikilink") on 17 February 2011. The play charted
    the deteriorating relationship between Signoret and [Marilyn
    Monroe](Marilyn_Monroe "wikilink") during the filming of *[Let\'s
    Make Love](Let's_Make_Love "wikilink")*. Unable to achieve the
    recognition of Oscar-winning Signoret, Monroe begins an affair with
    Signoret\'s husband, [Yves Montand](Yves_Montand "wikilink").
-   Singer [Nina Simone](Nina_Simone "wikilink") (Born Eunice Waymon)
    took her last name from Simone Signoret.[^44]
